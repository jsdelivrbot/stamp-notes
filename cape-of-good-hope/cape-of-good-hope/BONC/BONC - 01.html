Cape of Good Hope - Postal History

 
       

The Barred Oval Numeral Canceller 1864

 

The Barred Oval Numeral Canceller (BONC) was what is termed a 'dumb' canceller. It was intended to be used in conjunction with the circular datestamps, that incorporated the name of the post office from which mail was dispatched. The cancellers were manufactured in England and the design is similar to BONC cancellers used in Britain and it's colonies at the time.

The exceedingly rare BONC 227 used in Kimberley - Goldblatt comments .. only No. 227 is known and it was used in Kimberley. Only one example has been noted. Dr Y Lazarides collection.

The first issued cancellers were alloted to Cape Town and Port Elizabeth in 1864. By the following year the cancellers were distributed around the colony. By 1865, about 500 post offices and postal agencies in the Cape had been issued with the 'Thick Bar' Numberal Canceller of 1864. This distribution like it's earlier the octagonal stamp was indiscriminate. East London was given the No. 29, whereas the old established Cradock office received No. 226. No allocation lists appear to have been prepared and if they did no copies exist.

1894 Cover from Lady Grey addressed to Port Elizabeth bearing 1d red Seated Hope adhesive cancelled BONC 969. Adjacent “LADY GREY DIV. ALIWAL NORTH” circular date stamp (dot type) dated “3 AU 94”

The Thick Bar Numeral Cancellers supplied by the Cape of Good Hope postal administration authorties were also in use in several neighbouring Southern Africa territories. Again, there is no semblance of order in the allocation of te handstamps. In 1885, when the first postage stamps of British Bechuanaland were issued, it's gvernment was supplied by the Cape postalauthorities with circular stamps manufactured in Cape Town. At the same time, Barred Oval Numeral Cancellers of the 1864 type were sent to Kuruman (No. 534), Vryburg (555) and Mafeking (638).

As the cancellers worn out replacements and re-allocations were made. The No. 230 issued to Graaf-Reinet in 1880, shows the figure 3 with a round top, while the Britstown post office used a No. 230 in 1896 in which the 3 has a flat top. Sometimes the dimension of the oval proves that more than one issue was made.

In 1880, for example, the Wolvefontein Station post office used No. 218 with an oval measuring 21 by 29 mm and, in 1891, Steytlerville aso used a 218, the oval measuring 21 by 27 mm.

In many instances the cancellers were used concurrently with the triangular defacers. The use of the BONC cancellers extended well into the Union period and they have been noted as late as 1942.

Classification of Bar Oval Numeral Cancellers

The classification of the BONC cancellers presents a problem in that so many

 
 
different types were used. Goldblatt lists 48 different types and here we generally follow this principle. The BONC's can be firstly classified into broad categories (thick bar cancellers, thin bar cancellers, duplex cancellations and the Barred Circle Numeral Canceller). These categories can then be divided in groups and types if necessary. For example the first group, the Thick Bar Numeral Cancellers have been sub-divided into different types based on the number of bars present. These varied from nine bars for the first group to 14 bars for the last group. The normal practice of the industry is to refer to a BONC by the number present i.e BONC 1 would represent a Cape Town cancellation when referred to in a catalogue albeit not giving a description of it's type. A list of all numerals can be found here.

The Thick Bar Numeral Canceller

   

Nine Bar Cancellers (Group 1)

The first group is the Nine Bar Numeral Canceller (BONC 1 to 18). By far this is the most common of the many varieties. There are differences in the size and shape of the oval as well as the size of the numeral canceller.

The measurement of the oval is helpful in determining the date of issue. The table below indicates some of the probable dates of use based on the dimensions of the oval.

1864/5

Size of oval 21 by 28 mm.

1879

Size of oval 15.5 by 22 mm

1880

Size of oval 21 by 29 mm

All these varieties differ in the size of the oval and thickness of the numerals, as well as the shape and size of box enclosing the numeral. BONC 8, showing a stop after the last digit (66), was in use in Willomore in the Cape. The dot obviously having been added to distinguish from the number 99.

The Thirteen Bar Numeral Canceller (Group 4)

There are two types of the Thirteen Bar Canceller (see BONC 25 and BONC 26) the two types differing in dimensions of the ovals, the numeras and the box.

The Fourteen Bar Numeral Canceller (Group 5)

GROUP 5
BONC  27

The 14 bar Numeral Canceller has three thick bars at the top and bottom of the oval and four short, thick bars alternating with four short thin ones. This canceller (BONC 27) was evidently used in Grahmstown in the year 1894.

GROUP 6
BONC 28

The Horizontal Nine Bar Numeral Canceller (Group 6)

This canceller is easily distinguished by the fact that the cancellers are horizontal. They are oval with the bars being in a vertical direction(see BONC 28). This canceller was used exclusively in Cape Town. They were used for the cancellation of postal stationary and stamps used on circulars and newspapers

GROUP 7
BONC 29

The Horizontal Nine Bar Three Figure Numeral Canceller (Group 7)

This canceller (BONC 29) is extremely rare. Only one example is recorded by Goldblatt. It has the numeral 227 and it was used in Kimberley. See also an image of a strike on a postal wrapper at the beginning of this page.

GROUP 8
 

The Reversed Numeral Bar Cancellers (Group 8)

These cancellers show the serif of the one pointing backwards. They appear to have been used in Cape Town exclusively.They were used for the cancellation of postal stationary and stamps used on circulars and newspapers (see BONC 30 & 31).

GROUP 9
BONC 32

The Four-Figure Numeral Bar Cancellers (Group 9) 1896

The ninth and final group of the Tick Bar cancellers is the Four-figure Numeral Canceller of 1896. It started with the No 1000. This particular (BONC 32) was also distributed outside the Cape Colony.
